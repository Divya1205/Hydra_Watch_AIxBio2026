PATHOGENICITY-RELEVANT PROTEIN DOMAINS AND CONSERVATION ANALYSIS
=================================================================
Source: Based on CASPER study (medRxiv preprint, March 2026) and current scientific literature
Focus: Group A pathogens - highest pathogenicity (H5N1, SARS-CoV-2, Measles Virus, West Nile Virus)


NOTE ON PAPER CONTEXT
---------------------
The CASPER paper itself does not discuss protein domains or conservation degrees in detail.
H5N1 and SARS-CoV-2 are phylogenetically classified and analyzed at subtype level,
but no domain-specific pathogenicity discussion is provided. The following is based on
current scientific background knowledge from peer-reviewed literature.


================================================================================
1. H5N1 INFLUENZA A (RNA VIRUS, NEGATIVE-SENSE)
================================================================================

PATHOGENICITY-RELEVANT PROTEIN DOMAINS
---------------------------------------

a) Hemagglutinin (HA) - Receptor Binding Domain
   - Primary determinant of host range and cross-species transmissibility
   - Key mutations: Q226L, G228S, T199I
   - These mutations increase affinity for human-type (alpha-2,6-linked sialic acid) receptors
   - Avian strains preferentially bind alpha-2,3-linked sialic acid receptors
   - Single amino acid changes can dramatically shift receptor specificity

b) PB2 Polymerase Subunit - 627 Domain
   - Most critical pathogenicity determinant for mammalian adaptation
   - Key mutation: E627K (glutamate to lysine at position 627)
     → Primary factor for enhanced polymerase activity in human cells
     → Identified in first human H5N1 isolate from US dairy worker (Texas, April 2024)
   - Additional key mutation: D701N
     → Mammalian adaptation marker; absence associated with low pathogenicity
   - Both E627K and D701N serve as markers for mammalian adaptation

c) PB2 - Additional Critical Positions
   - M631L mutation: enhances polymerase activity in mammalian cells
     → Previously co-occurred in H5N1 outbreaks in Cambodia/Vietnam 2013-2014
     → Present in >99% of bovine H5N1 sequences from 2024-2025
   - E362G: contributes to enhanced polymerase activity (lesser extent than E627K)
   - S181 phosphorylation site: highly conserved across IAV strains, restricts virulence
   - K187 acetylation site: conserved, influences viral fitness

d) Nucleoprotein (NP)
   - Key mutations: PB2-478I and NP-450N
   - Associated with enhanced immune cell tropism and systemic pathogenicity
   - Enable viral dissemination via immune cells (neuroinvasion)
   - Found in 95-97% of recent mammalian and human H5N1 isolates

e) PB1-F2 Accessory Protein
   - Contributes to pathogenicity via mitochondrial membrane disruption
   - Promotes apoptosis in immune cells
   - Variable across strains; N66S mutation associated with increased virulence

f) Neuraminidase (NA)
   - Key mutations for drug resistance: H274Y (oseltamivir resistance), S137A, I222V
   - Functional cleavage of sialic acid essential for viral release from cells

CONSERVATION DEGREES
--------------------
Within H5N1 strains:
  - HA receptor binding domain: functionally conserved but harbors critical point mutations
    that determine host tropism (high variability at key positions)
  - PB2 core structure: highly conserved; adaptation mutations (E627K, D701N) strain-specific
  - NP: relatively conserved core; pathogenicity mutations accumulated in mammalian lineages

Between Influenza A subtypes:
  - PB2 basic structure: highly conserved across all IAV subtypes
  - HA head domain: highly variable (basis for H1-H18 subtype classification)
  - Polymerase complex (PB1, PB2, PA): structurally conserved, functionally essential motifs

2. SARS-CoV-2 (RNA VIRUS, POSITIVE-SENSE)
================================================================================

PATHOGENICITY-RELEVANT PROTEIN DOMAINS
---------------------------------------

a) Spike Protein - Receptor Binding Domain (RBD)
   - Binds human ACE2 receptor; primary determinant of infectivity
   - Mutations in RBD residues increase binding affinity to ACE2
   - Key stabilizing mutations (Omicron): G339D, S371L, N440K, S477N, T478K,
     E484A, Q493R, Q498R, Y505H
   - D614G mutation: became dominant early in pandemic; enhances spike trimer
     stability and viral infectivity

b) Spike S1/S2 Cleavage Site - Furin Binding Site
   - Polybasic furin cleavage site: unique to SARS-CoV-2 among SARS-related coronaviruses
   - Enables efficient cleavage by host furin protease → enhanced cell entry
   - P681R mutation (Delta): increases fusogenicity → higher pathogenicity
   - Q677H mutation: located in polybasic region, influences S1/S2 cleavage

c) Spike S2 Subunit - Fusion Machinery
   - Mediates fusion of viral envelope with host cell membrane
   - Contains: fusion peptide (FP), heptad repeat regions (HR1, HR2),
     central helix (CH), connector domain (CD)
   - Less mutation-prone than S1 → more stable therapeutic target
   - Hinge regions in stalk: highly conserved among SARS coronaviruses

d) RdRp Complex (NSP12/NSP7/NSP8) - RNA Replication
   - NSP12: catalytic subunit; contains NiRAN domain, interface domain, RdRp domain
   - P323L mutation in NSP12: highly conserved across all five VOCs
     → Stabilizes NSP12-NSP7-NSP8 complex; enhances RdRp activity
   - NSP7/NSP8: accessory factors enhancing template binding and processivity
   - Conserved polymerase motifs A, B, F, G: essential for RNA synthesis

e) Non-Structural Proteins (Accessory Pathogenicity Factors)
   - NSP3 + NSP4 + NSP6: biogenesis of replication organelle
   - NSP6 + ORF7a: ubiquitination promotes NF-κB pathway activation (inflammation)
   - NSP12 P323L/G671S: stabilize replication complex across VOCs

f) Nucleocapsid Protein (N)
   - RNA encapsidation; involved in immune evasion
   - Multiple variants carry mutations that affect innate immune response modulation

CONSERVATION DEGREES
--------------------
Within SARS-CoV-2 variants:
  - RdRp (NSP12) core domains: very highly conserved across all variants
  - Spike RBD: highly variable (immune evasion driver; basis for variant classification)
  - S2 fusion domain: relatively conserved compared to S1
  - P323L in NSP12: conserved across all VOCs (Alpha, Beta, Delta, Omicron, etc.)

Between Betacoronaviruses:
  - S2 fusion domain and RdRp core motifs: conserved
  - S1/RBD: considerably divergent between SARS-CoV, SARS-CoV-2, MERS-CoV
  - Six conserved structural proteins (pp1a, pp1ab, S, E, M, N): present across coronaviruses

3. MEASLES VIRUS (RNA VIRUS, NEGATIVE-SENSE)
================================================================================

PATHOGENICITY-RELEVANT PROTEIN DOMAINS
---------------------------------------

a) Hemagglutinin (H Protein) - Receptor Binding Site
   - Binds two principal cellular receptors:
     → SLAM (CD150/Signaling Lymphocyte Activation Molecule): on immune cells
     → Nectin-4: at adherens junctions of epithelial cells
   - Receptor binding site itself forms a conserved neutralizing epitope (CNE)
   - Because receptor binding is essential for pathogenesis, viral escape from
     neutralization cannot occur without loss of receptor-binding activity
   - H protein determines viral tropism

b) H Protein Stalk - Fusion Activation Domain
   - 96-residue stalk transmits receptor-binding signal to F protein
   - Residues 86-109: highly conserved among morbilliviruses; central signal
     transmission region
   - HR-like domain (I84-L105): contains completely conserved proline at position 94
     and conserved leucine at position 95 across paramyxoviruses
   - Heptadic residues (I84, V91, I98, L105): mutations here severely reduce fusion activity
   - The stalk, not the globular head, is the effector domain for F protein triggering

c) Fusion Protein (F Protein) - Membrane Fusion
   - Mediates virion-to-cell and cell-to-cell membrane fusion
   - Produced as non-functional precursor, cleaved by furin protease into F1 and F2
   - Prefusion conformation is metastable; destabilization → hyperfusogenic phenotype
   - L454W mutation in F protein: enables fusion without H protein → neuroinvasion
     (found in patients with SSPE/MIBE - fatal CNS complications)
   - Inhibitor binding site: hydrophobic pocket connecting head and stalk regions
   - F protein is the target for fusion inhibitor drug development

d) Nucleocapsid Protein (N)
   - Large conserved core domain: supports self-assembly and RNA binding
   - Hypervariable C-terminal tail: intrinsically disordered; required for RNA synthesis
   - N protein is tightly associated with genomic RNA

e) Phosphoprotein (P) and V/C Proteins
   - P protein: essential polymerase cofactor; C-terminal half most conserved
   - Contains polymerase-binding region and RNP-binding sequences
   - V protein: IFN antagonist; zinc-binding C-terminal domain
   - C protein: modulates immune evasion and pathogenicity

CONSERVATION DEGREES
--------------------
Within measles virus strains:
  - F protein: highly conserved; major variations only at positions 451, 467, 471
  - H protein head domain: more variable (basis for 24 genotype classification)
  - H stalk (residues 86-109): very highly conserved among morbilliviruses
  - N protein core domain: highly conserved

Between morbilliviruses (MeV, CDV, RPV, PPRV):
  - F protein: highly conserved across all morbilliviruses
  - Key structural features of H stalk: conserved (4-helix bundle organization)
  - Central elements of F-triggering machinery: highly conserved within paramyxovirus family

4. WEST NILE VIRUS (RNA VIRUS, POSITIVE-SENSE)
================================================================================

PATHOGENICITY-RELEVANT PROTEIN DOMAINS
---------------------------------------

a) Envelope Protein (E) - Host Cell Entry
   - Three structural domains: DI (central), DII (dimerization/fusion loop), DIII (receptor binding)
   - Domain III (DIII): primary determinant of receptor binding and neuroinvasiveness
   - N-Y-S glycosylation motif in E protein: associated with increased pathogenicity;
     present in all Australian isolates since the 1980s
   - Amino acid at position 156: involved in plaque size, growth, and pathogenicity
   - Domain II fusion loop: highly conserved across flaviviruses; mediates membrane fusion

b) NS1 Protein
   - Secreted and membrane-associated forms
   - Key role in immune evasion and complement modulation
   - NS1-175 and NS1-130: associated with virulence differences between strains

c) NS3 - Protease and Helicase Domain
   - Dual enzymatic function:
     → Protease domain (N-terminal): processes viral polyprotein (with NS2B cofactor)
     → Helicase domain (C-terminal): unwinds dsRNA replication intermediate
   - Seven NS3 residues identified as critical for viral replication; well conserved
     among mosquito-borne flaviviruses
   - Less efficient NS3 cleavage → delayed virus assembly → more time for immune clearance
   - NS3-NS5 protein-protein interaction surface: potential drug target

d) NS4B
   - Component of the viral replicase complex
   - A79T mutation: alters hydrophobicity → structural/functional implications for
     virulence differences between strains
   - NS4A/NS4B: membrane rearrangements for replication complex formation

e) NS5 - RNA-Dependent RNA Polymerase and Methyltransferase
   - Most variable protein among WNV strains of differing virulence
   - Contains RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RdRp) domain: functionally essential,
     conserved core motifs
   - N-terminal methyltransferase domain: involved in 5' cap synthesis
   - Phenylalanine at position 653: present in all virulent isolates; associated with
     pathogenicity
   - NS5-V258A, NS5-N280K, NS5-A372V, NS5-R422K: associated with attenuated phenotypes

CONSERVATION DEGREES
--------------------
Within WNV lineages:
  - E protein domain II fusion loop: highly conserved
  - E protein domain III: moderately conserved; key pathogenicity determinants variable
  - NS3 protease/helicase core: structurally conserved; virulence differences via
    specific point mutations
  - NS5 polymerase: most variable protein; conserved catalytic core motifs

Between flaviviruses (WNV, DENV, Zika, JEV):
  - NS3 residues critical for replication: well conserved among mosquito-borne flaviviruses
  - Pan-WNV sequences show partial overlap with dengue virus sequences (3-100%)
    → variable but sometimes significant cross-reactivity between flaviviruses
  - E protein overall structure: conserved three-domain architecture; DII fusion loop
    highly conserved across genus Flavivirus

================================================================================
KEY BIOLOGICAL INSIGHT
================================================================================

A common pattern emerges across all four viruses:

VARIABLE REGIONS (receptor binding domains):
  → Subject to immune evasion pressure
  → Enable host range expansion / cross-species transmission
  → Drive variant/strain diversification

CONSERVED REGIONS (replication machinery, fusion proteins):
  → Functionally essential; mutations here often lethal for virus
  → Less immune pressure → maintained across strains
  → Prime targets for broad-spectrum antiviral drug development

This pattern is directly relevant for surveillance strategies like CASPER:
Deep metagenomic sequencing can detect both variable regions (for strain typing)
and conserved regions (for pathogen identification), making the untargeted
approach superior to PCR-based methods that target only pre-defined sequences.
